SECTION 1: GENERAL RISK FACTORS (ALL ISSUERS)
=============================================================================

RISK FACTOR 1.1 — BUSINESS AND MARKET RISK
Our business is dependent on general economic conditions in India and globally.
The performance of our business is linked to economic growth, consumer spending, interest rates, inflation, and capital market conditions in India. A downturn in the Indian economy or a global recession could adversely affect our revenues, profitability, and financial condition. GDP growth deceleration, rising inflation, or tightening monetary policy could reduce demand for our products/services. We cannot assure investors that historical growth rates in our business will continue.

RISK FACTOR 1.2 — REGULATORY AND COMPLIANCE RISK
We operate in a regulated industry and our business is subject to various laws, regulations, and government policies that may change without notice.
Changes in government regulations, tax laws, import duties, environmental regulations, or sector-specific policies could increase our compliance costs, restrict our operations, or adversely affect our profitability. Failure to obtain, maintain, or renew necessary licenses, permits, or approvals could materially disrupt our operations. Non-compliance with applicable laws could result in penalties, sanctions, or cancellation of our operating licenses.

RISK FACTOR 1.3 — COMPETITION RISK
We operate in a highly competitive market and competition may intensify in the future.
We face competition from established players with greater financial resources, brand recognition, distribution networks, and technological capabilities. New entrants, including foreign companies backed by private equity or venture capital, may enter our market with competitive pricing or superior technology. Any failure to successfully compete could result in loss of market share, reduction in revenue, and compression of margins.

RISK FACTOR 1.4 — KEY PERSONNEL RISK
Our business is dependent on the continued services of our promoters and key management personnel.
Our operations, customer relationships, and strategic direction are heavily dependent on the continued involvement of our promoters and key management personnel. The loss of any one of them, without adequate replacement, could disrupt our business and adversely affect our financial performance. We cannot assure that our promoters and KMPs will continue to serve the company post-IPO. We do not maintain key man insurance for all key personnel.

RISK FACTOR 1.5 — WORKING CAPITAL RISK
Our business requires significant working capital and any inability to manage working capital efficiently could adversely affect our operations.
Our business involves maintaining adequate inventory, extending trade credit to customers, and managing vendor payment terms. Any elongation in our debtor collection period, inventory pile-up, or reduction in supplier credit could create cash flow stress. We have relied on bank credit facilities and internal accruals to fund working capital. If our working capital requirements increase significantly or our existing facilities are not renewed, our operations may be adversely affected.

RISK FACTOR 1.6 — PROMOTER CONFLICT OF INTEREST
Our Promoters are involved in other businesses and there may be potential conflicts of interest.
Our Promoters are interested in other entities engaged in businesses that may be similar to or compete with our business. There can be no assurance that our Promoters will not favour such other entities over us when business opportunities arise. We have not entered into formal non-compete agreements with our Promoters. Such conflicts of interest could adversely affect our competitive position and profitability.

RISK FACTOR 1.7 — LITIGATION RISK
We are party to certain legal proceedings, the adverse outcome of which could affect our financial condition.
We are involved in certain legal proceedings including civil suits, tax disputes, and labour matters. If determined adversely against us, such proceedings could require us to pay material damages or penalties. The amount of contingent liabilities may not be fully reflected in our financial statements. Litigation is time-consuming and costly, and its resolution is uncertain.

RISK FACTOR 1.8 — TECHNOLOGY AND CYBERSECURITY RISK
Our business depends on information technology systems and any failure, breach, or disruption could materially harm our operations.
We rely on IT systems for core operations including order management, production planning, financial reporting, and customer management. Any failure, cyber-attack, data breach, or prolonged system downtime could disrupt operations, result in data loss, cause regulatory non-compliance, and damage our reputation. The increasing sophistication of cyber threats and our possible inadequate cybersecurity measures pose significant risks to our business.

RISK FACTOR 1.9 — DEBT AND LEVERAGE RISK
Our indebtedness could adversely affect our financial condition and ability to service our obligations.
We have significant outstanding borrowings from banks and financial institutions. Our financing arrangements impose various restrictions including maintenance of financial ratios, creation of security over assets, and restrictions on dividend payment. Our inability to service debt obligations, or any breach of covenants, could result in acceleration of repayment obligations and seizure of collateral. High leverage limits our operational flexibility and restricts our ability to raise additional capital.

RISK FACTOR 1.10 — FOREIGN EXCHANGE RISK
We are exposed to foreign exchange risks on account of imports, exports, and foreign currency borrowings.
[APPLICABLE WHERE RELEVANT] We import raw materials and/or export products, making our margins sensitive to fluctuations in the Indian Rupee against the US Dollar, Euro, and other currencies. While we may enter into hedging arrangements, we cannot guarantee complete coverage against exchange rate movements. Adverse currency movements could increase our input costs or reduce our export realisations.

SECTION 2: MANUFACTURING SECTOR RISK FACTORS
=============================================================================

RISK FACTOR 2.1 — RAW MATERIAL AVAILABILITY AND PRICE
Volatility in raw material prices and supply disruptions could adversely affect our margins.
Raw materials (specify: steel, aluminium, plastics, chemicals, cotton, etc.) constitute approximately X% of our total production cost. We are exposed to fluctuations in raw material prices driven by global commodity markets, seasonal demand, logistics disruptions, and government policies including export restrictions. We do not maintain long-term fixed-price contracts with all our suppliers, and any significant increase in raw material prices, without corresponding increase in product prices, will compress our operating margins. Major supply disruptions could result in production downtime.

RISK FACTOR 2.2 — CAPACITY UTILIZATION RISK
Our profitability depends on achieving adequate capacity utilization at our manufacturing facilities.
Our manufacturing operations involve significant fixed costs (depreciation, employee costs, utilities). If our production volumes decline due to market slowdown, customer concentration risk, or product quality issues, our fixed cost absorption will reduce and our margins will be adversely affected. Our manufacturing plants are currently operating at X% utilization. We cannot assure that we will maintain or improve this level.

RISK FACTOR 2.3 — ENVIRONMENTAL COMPLIANCE
Our manufacturing operations are subject to environmental laws and regulations.
Our manufacturing processes generate effluents, air emissions, and solid waste that are subject to environmental laws and pollution control regulations. Any failure to comply with applicable environmental standards could result in regulatory action, shutdown notices, or financial penalties. Environmental compliance costs may increase over time as regulations become more stringent. We are also exposed to liability for any historical contamination at our manufacturing sites.

RISK FACTOR 2.4 — POWER AND UTILITY RISK
Our manufacturing operations are dependent on reliable power supply.
Our manufacturing processes are energy-intensive and any shortage, price increase, or unreliable supply of electricity could adversely affect our production volumes and costs. We operate in states where power infrastructure may be inadequate, and power cuts or load shedding during peak demand periods are common. While we have captive diesel generator backup, prolonged power outages would increase our energy costs significantly.

RISK FACTOR 2.5 — PRODUCT LIABILITY RISK
We are exposed to product liability claims if our products fail to meet quality standards.
Any defects in our products could result in personal injury, property damage, or financial losses to our customers, leading to product recalls, warranty claims, or litigation. Such events could damage our reputation, result in financial penalties, and adversely affect our business. While we maintain product liability insurance, such coverage may not be adequate to cover all potential claims.

RISK FACTOR 3.1 — CUSTOMER CONCENTRATION RISK (IT)
We derive a significant portion of our revenues from a limited number of customers.
Our top 5 customers account for approximately X% of our total revenues. Loss of any key customer, renegotiation of pricing, reduction in order volumes, or termination of contracts by any major customer could have a material adverse effect on our business. We may not be able to replace such revenues in the short term. Customer contracts typically include termination for convenience clauses.

RISK FACTOR 3.2 — TECHNOLOGY OBSOLESCENCE RISK
Rapid technological changes could render our services or solutions obsolete.
The IT industry is characterised by rapid technological change, including the emergence of cloud computing, artificial intelligence, machine learning, and quantum computing. If we fail to adapt our service offerings, tools, and methodologies to new technologies, our competitiveness could diminish. Investment in technology upgradation and R&D is continuous and significant.

RISK FACTOR 3.3 — TALENT ATTRITION RISK
High employee attrition in the IT industry may disrupt our operations.
The IT industry experiences high attrition rates of 15-25% annually. Our business depends on retaining experienced technical personnel. Loss of skilled employees to competitors, startups, or foreign employers could disrupt project delivery, increase recruitment and training costs, and affect client relationships. We compete for talent with larger Indian IT companies and multinational corporations.

RISK FACTOR 3.4 — VISA AND IMMIGRATION RISK (EXPORT-ORIENTED IT)
Changes in visa regulations in key markets could limit our ability to serve clients onsite.
Our business model involves deploying resources at client sites in the US, Europe, and other geographies. Restrictive visa policies, reduced visa quotas, or increased visa rejection rates could limit our onsite delivery capacity. Changes in H-1B visa policies in the US have historically affected Indian IT companies. Any such restrictions could increase our offshore delivery costs and reduce client satisfaction.

RISK FACTOR 4.1 — ASSET QUALITY RISK
Deterioration in asset quality could adversely affect our financial performance.
Our loan portfolio is exposed to credit risk from borrower defaults, sector-specific downturns, natural disasters, and macroeconomic headwinds. Our Gross NPA (Non-Performing Assets) ratio as of [date] is X%. Any material increase in NPAs would require higher provisioning, reduce profitability, and impair our capital adequacy. Our lending to Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs), self-employed individuals, or rural segments carries inherently higher credit risk.

RISK FACTOR 4.2 — REGULATORY RISK (NBFC)
Changes in RBI regulations governing NBFCs could adversely affect our business.
We are registered with the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) as a Non-Banking Financial Company (NBFC). RBI regulations regarding capital adequacy, asset classification, provisioning norms, interest rate caps, customer protection, and fair lending practices could change. Enhanced regulatory scrutiny, inspections, or adverse findings from RBI could restrict our operations or result in penalties.

RISK FACTOR 4.3 — FUNDING RISK
Our business depends on access to adequate and cost-effective funding.
We fund our loan portfolio through bank borrowings, NCDs, and securitisation. Any disruption in funding access, increase in borrowing costs, or reduction in credit limits could impair our ability to grow or even sustain our lending operations. Lenders may impose restrictive covenants. Our credit ratings, if downgraded, would increase our borrowing costs significantly.

RISK FACTOR 4.4 — INTEREST RATE RISK
Changes in interest rates could adversely affect our net interest margin.
Our net interest margin (NIM) is sensitive to movements in benchmark interest rates (Repo Rate, MCLR). An increase in our cost of funds, without corresponding increase in lending rates, would compress NIM. Conversely, if we increase lending rates to protect NIM, we risk higher NPAs from borrowers unable to service higher EMIs.

SECTION 5: RETAIL AND FMCG SECTOR RISK FACTORS
=============================================================================

RISK FACTOR 5.1 — DISTRIBUTION AND SUPPLY CHAIN RISK
Our business relies on a large and geographically dispersed distribution network.
We distribute our products through a network of distributors, wholesale traders, and retail outlets. Any disruption in this network due to disputes with distributors, logistics failures, or transportation strikes could interrupt product availability and adversely affect our sales. We are exposed to the risk of damaged goods, expired inventory, and counterfeit products in the distribution chain.

RISK FACTOR 5.2 — BRAND RISK
Our business depends on the strength and perception of our brand.
Our revenues and market position depend significantly on consumer perception of our brand. Negative publicity, product recalls, quality complaints, or social media criticism could damage our brand equity. Counterfeit products bearing our brand name could mislead consumers and erode trust. Rebuilding brand reputation after a negative event could require significant time and investment.

RISK FACTOR 5.3 — SEASONAL DEMAND RISK
Our sales exhibit significant seasonal variation.
Our product categories experience peak demand during festivals, summer seasons, or specific agricultural cycles. In off-peak quarters, our inventory and working capital requirements remain high while revenues decline. This seasonality affects our quarterly cash flows and makes it difficult to maintain uniform operational efficiency throughout the year.

SECTION 6: HEALTHCARE AND PHARMA SECTOR RISK FACTORS
=============================================================================

RISK FACTOR 6.1 — REGULATORY APPROVAL RISK (PHARMA)
Our products require regulatory approvals from CDSCO, FSSAI, and other authorities.
Manufacturing and sale of pharmaceutical, nutraceutical, or medical device products is subject to approvals from the Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation (CDSCO), State Drug Controllers, FSSAI, and other regulatory bodies. Any failure to obtain, maintain, or renew approvals, or any adverse inspection findings, could suspend our manufacturing or sales activities.

RISK FACTOR 6.2 — DRUG PRICE CONTROL RISK
Our products may be subject to drug price controls under DPCO.
Pharmaceutical products listed in the National List of Essential Medicines (NLEM) are subject to price control under the Drug Price Control Order (DPCO). Any expansion of DPCO coverage to include our key products would reduce our pricing flexibility and compress margins.

RISK FACTOR 6.3 — QUALITY AND GMP COMPLIANCE RISK
Non-compliance with Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) could lead to product recalls or plant shutdown.
Our manufacturing facilities must comply with GMP guidelines issued by WHO, CDSCO, and export market regulators (USFDA, EU GMP). Any inspection observations, Warning Letters, or Import Alerts could result in product recalls, export bans, plant shutdowns, and significant financial losses. Remediation of GMP deficiencies is time-consuming and expensive.
